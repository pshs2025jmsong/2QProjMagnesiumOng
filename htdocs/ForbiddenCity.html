Forbidden City



The Forbidden City is a palace complex located in the Dongcheng District, Beijing, China. Located in the center of the Imperial City of Beijing, it is surrounded by luxurious imperial gardens and temples.

Constructed from 1406 to 1420, the Forbidden City was a Chinese imperial palace for the Chinese emperor from the Ming dynasty to the end of the Qing dynasty, which was from 1420 to 1924. The Forbidden City served as the home of several Chinese emperors. It was also the ceremonial and political center of the Chinese government for more than five centuries.

The Forbidden City was declared as a World Heritage Site in 1987.

The complex consists of 980 buildings, containing 8,886 rooms and covering about 720,000 square meters. The palace perfectly shows the affluence of the of the residences of the Chinese emperor. The palace also influenced cultural and architectural developments in East Asia. UNESCO listed the palace as the largest collection of preserved ancient wooden structures in the world.

History



The construction of the Forbidden City began in 1406 when Hongwu Emperor's son Zhu Di became the Yongle Emperor and moved the capital from Nanjing to Beijing.

Previously home to 24 emperors, 14 of the Ming dynasty and 10 of the Qing dynasty, the Forbidden City ended its reputation being the political center of China in 1912 due to the abdication of Puyi, the last Emperor of China. An agreement under the new Republic of China government states that Puyi would remain in the Inner Court while the Outer Court was given over to public use. In 1924, Puyi was evicted after a coup. A year later, the Palace Museum would be established in the Forbidden City.

In 1933, the Japanese invaded China, which forced the evacuation of the national treasures in the Forbidden City. A portion of the treasures were returned after World War II, while the other part was kept in Taiwan under orders of Chiang Kai-shek, whose political party was losing the Chinese Civil War. The treasures kept in Taiwan remained there until 1965 and became the core of the National Palace Museum in Taipei.

In 1949, the establishment of People's Republic of China caused a revolutionary zeal, which caused some damage to the Forbidden City. During the Cultural Revolution, further destruction was prevented when an army battalion was sent by Premier Zhou Enlai to guard the city.

In 1987, the Forbidden City was declared a World Heritage Site by UNESCO. The Forbidden City is currently administered by the Palace Museum, which is carrying out a restoration to restore all buildings to their pre-1912 state.

Structure



The Forbidden City is a rectangular palace complex, measuring 961 meters (3,153 ft) from north to south and 753 meters (2,470 ft) from east to west. It is comprised of 980 (survived) buildings and 8,886 bays of rooms. It was also designed to be the center of the ancient, walled city of Beijing, which is enclosed in a larger, walled area called the Imperial City. The Imperial City is enclosed by the Inner City, where to its south lies the Outer City.
